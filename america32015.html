America III

To close out my three-week trip across the country, I planned out some stops in one of my favorite parts of the country, the Pacific Northwest, before working my way down to San Francisco in a leisurely and touristy fashion. The week began with a long and incredibly tranquil drive from Missoula to Seattle. Out of all the stretches I drove across all of America, this leg just might have been my favorite. Maybe tied for first. I quickly hit the winding, climbing roads of Idaho and stopped for lakeside coffee in stunning Coeur d’Alene, which felt slightly reminiscent of the Burlington waterfront. “I could live here,” I remember thinking to myself. Does anyone know a mine that’s hiring?

Then began the drive across Washington. The mountains gave way to rolling hills and brown farmland where cattle grazed so freely I’d drive miles between seeing a couple cows and seeing some kind of barn structure (any structure at all, really). I saw crop dusters looping over fields at insane speeds and low altitude (google this—it’s like plane NASCAR). Crossing the enormous Columbia River in Vantage marked my halfway point, so I stopped for photos. I made it to Seattle just in time to reunite with my close college friend David at the Seattle Bouldering Project.

The next morning, I set out early to meet up with my dad in Portland. We decided to get out of the city for the early part of the day and drove a scenic byway down to the Columbia River Gorge and nearby waterfalls. It was incredible scenery for being half an hour outside of Portland, and I’d absolutely consider it a must-do for anyone visiting the area. To refuel after hours of looking at stuff and snapping photos (grueling), we headed into the Pearl District and got beers and salmon at the Deschutes brew pub.

The following two days were spent meandering down the California coastline (split up with a quick night in Eureka, an underrated town with lots of great beer, food, and wilderness nearby) via Avenue of the Giants, Highway 1, and the so appropriately dubbed Lost Coast. The views were amazing and rugged, even through the smoke of the Valley Blaze, and the drive passed in no time (at least for me…I wasn’t the one driving. Thanks, Dad!). Before I knew it, we were crossing the Golden Gate Bridge in cold, dense fog, which we both understood to be our official welcome to our final destination.
